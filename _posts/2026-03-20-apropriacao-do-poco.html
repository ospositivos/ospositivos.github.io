seis dias de guerra vs um ano de salários ou um milhão de casas

Conhecem-nos hipótese: civilizações vêm(-se), civilizações vão-se. É um ciclo, está nos ecrãs, livros, papiros, tabuletas de barro, menires, ossos. Confluência de crises, todas elas — inclusive as ambientais e pandémicas — exacerbadas pelo fosso entre elites ricas que se guerreiam e massas pobres sobre-exploradas, seguida do colapso da ordem antiga. Sabem-nos também onde achamos que estamos nesse ciclo (*).

*) E que é no intervalo deles que se está bem mas desses não reza a História: quem se dá ao trabalho de registar cronologia de posses à posteridade quando tudo é "gentil & justo"? Quando o mundo é belo, faz-se arte pelo prazer de fazer, deita-se fora e faz-se outra vez, talvez partilhes com os amigos.

Das muitas maleitas particulares da nossa circunstância, são várias, a do petróleo está na ordem do dia, segway a mashup do nosso jornalista habitual de causas ecológicas. Nada de novo abaixo do sol, mas iremos sempre dar preferência à comunicação social sobre os mesmos dizeres em literatura especializada por canais de erudição académica ou militância partidária que já pregam aos convertidos e fora desses podiam apelar ignorância. É-nos mesmo deli-doce destacar informação acessível, parte do discurso público, narrativas da cultura popular, mais passivo-agressivo ainda se a podermos divulgar no mais rasteiro dos formatos: comix aos teens. Senhores, remisturado à vossa atenção,

I realise this is a serious breach of etiquette.

As the hydrocarbon industries and their financial backers find themselves threatened by green technologies, their grip on governments and the media has tightened. The democratic recession is in large part driven by fossil fuel interests. Control over resources is the driving force of politics. Democracy, at the moment, is the lightshow played on the castle walls.

The world’s military power exists in large part to deliver the profit from resources – especially oil – to banks and shareholders, commodity traders and asset managers, hedge funds and private equity companies. For the same purpose, the infrastructure of persuasion – lobbyists, media, social media algorithms – is mobilised to ensure the most amoral, sadistic and bellicose people are selected as leaders, as they will keep oil and other commodities flowing for the benefit of capital, whatever the human cost may be. Their opponents are demonised, alternatives dismissed as “unrealistic”, “unpopular” and “unaffordable”.

Take a step back and you see something that should be obvious. The conflation of capitalism with “free markets” is one of the most successful lies in human history. The historical and ongoing plunder of resources; the police, armies and death squads deployed against those who resist; the intimidation of labour; the conning of consumers; the extraction of rent; the dumping of costs on the living planet: all this is the opposite of “free”. It’s highly coercive and extremely expensive. Much of the time there’s little sign of a market, either. Land, commodities and labour are, in many cases, simply stolen. Public resources, whether oil reserves, forests, water systems or railways are given (or sold at a fraction of their value) to private monopolists. The rich are bailed out by the state when they run into trouble, while the poor must sink or swim. “Free market capitalism” is a contradiction in terms.

Concentrated fossil power leads to concentrated political power. Had we been less dependent on fossil fuels, there might have been no President Trump, no President Putin, no ayatollahs, no Prime Minister Netanyahu. Fossil fuels push the world towards autocracy. Overthrow our demand for them, and we overthrow much of the current tyranny. Greener, cleaner, cheaper, kinder, fairer: what a beautiful world we could have.
