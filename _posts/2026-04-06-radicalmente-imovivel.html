In their book David Graeber and David Wengrow highlight a significant and frequently overlooked historical phenomenon: during the colonial era, European settlers often ran to join Native American communities, while very few Indigenous people chose to permanently move into European settlements, [citing] accounts of European settlers —both captured and those who joined voluntarily— who were given the choice to stay with Indigenous societies or return to European colonies, and who almost invariably chose to stay with the indigenous populations. Even after being returned to European society, many settlers would take the first opportunity to escape and return to the "woods". Conversely, few Indigenous individuals who were "educated" by white settlers chose to stay in European society, often returning to their own people because they were unable to integrate or preferred their original ways of life.

This phenomenon serves as a central argument for the authors, demonstrating a preference for Indigenous societies' freedom, equality, and social structure over European hierarchy, punitive law, and competitive individualism. European society at the time was highly authoritarian, unequal, and maintained order through rigid punitive laws. Indigenous societies, in contrast, operated with a high degree of personal freedom and mutual aid. Indigenous observers [like the Wendat leader Kondiaronk] argued that European society was obsessed with material possessions, resulting in a lack of generosity, ease, and personal comfort [while] many Indigenous groups maintained social order through social cohesion and mutual respect, rather than the fear of prisons or executioners.

Graeber and Wengrow use this evidence to challenge the conventional "Standard Narrative" of history that views Western civilization as the pinnacle of progress. The widespread preference for Native life suggests that many Enlightenment ideas about liberty and equality were inspired by critiques from Native American leaders who observed the failures of European social organization.
